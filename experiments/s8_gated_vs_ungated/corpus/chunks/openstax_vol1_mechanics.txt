=== CHUNK 1: Kinematics and Newton's Laws ===

Position, Displacement, and Velocity
The position of an object is its location relative to a chosen reference point. Displacement is the change in position: Δx = x_f − x_i. Velocity is the rate of change of position. The instantaneous velocity is the derivative of position with respect to time: v = dx/dt.

Average velocity over an interval is v_avg = Δx/Δt. Speed is the magnitude of velocity and does not include direction. An object moving at constant velocity has zero acceleration. Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity: a = dv/dt = d²x/dt².

For constant acceleration a, the kinematic equations are:
  v = v₀ + at
  x = x₀ + v₀t + (1/2)at²
  v² = v₀² + 2a(x − x₀)

Newton's First Law states that an object at rest stays at rest and an object in motion stays in motion with constant velocity unless acted upon by a net external force. This is the law of inertia. An inertial reference frame is one in which Newton's first law holds.

Newton's Second Law states that the net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration: F_net = ma. Force is measured in newtons (N), where 1 N = 1 kg·m/s². This is a vector equation: the acceleration is in the direction of the net force.

Newton's Third Law states that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts a force of equal magnitude and opposite direction on object A: F_AB = −F_BA. These forces act on different objects and therefore do not cancel.

=== CHUNK 2: Energy and Work ===

Work and Kinetic Energy
Work is done when a force acts on an object and the object moves in the direction of the force. For a constant force F acting over a displacement d, the work is W = F·d = Fd cos θ, where θ is the angle between the force and displacement vectors. Work is measured in joules (J), where 1 J = 1 N·m.

The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy: W_net = ΔK = (1/2)mv_f² − (1/2)mv_i². Kinetic energy is the energy of motion: K = (1/2)mv².

Potential energy is stored energy. Gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface is U_g = mgh, where h is the height above a reference level and g = 9.80 m/s² is the acceleration due to gravity. The work done by gravity is W_g = −ΔU_g = −mgΔh.

Conservation of Mechanical Energy
When only conservative forces (gravity, springs) do work, the total mechanical energy is conserved: K_i + U_i = K_f + U_f. A conservative force is one for which the work done is independent of the path taken. Gravity and spring forces are conservative; friction is non-conservative.

The spring force is given by Hooke's Law: F = −kx, where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement from equilibrium. The elastic potential energy stored in a spring is U_s = (1/2)kx².

Power is the rate at which work is done: P = dW/dt. Average power is P_avg = W/Δt. Power is measured in watts (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.

=== CHUNK 3: Momentum and Collisions ===

Linear Momentum
The linear momentum of an object is the product of its mass and velocity: p = mv. Momentum is a vector quantity measured in kg·m/s. Newton's second law can be expressed in terms of momentum: F_net = dp/dt. The net external force equals the rate of change of momentum.

Impulse is the change in momentum: J = Δp = ∫ F dt. For a constant force, J = F_avg Δt. The impulse-momentum theorem states that the impulse on an object equals its change in momentum.

Conservation of Momentum
In an isolated system (no net external force), the total linear momentum is conserved: p_total,i = p_total,f. This is one of the most fundamental conservation laws in physics. It holds for all types of collisions.

Types of Collisions
In an elastic collision, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. For two objects: m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i = m₁v₁f + m₂v₂f AND (1/2)m₁v₁i² + (1/2)m₂v₂i² = (1/2)m₁v₁f² + (1/2)m₂v₂f².

In an inelastic collision, momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not. In a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together after the collision and move with a common velocity. The maximum kinetic energy loss occurs in perfectly inelastic collisions.

Center of Mass
The center of mass of a system of particles is the weighted average of their positions: r_cm = (Σ m_i r_i) / (Σ m_i). The velocity of the center of mass is v_cm = (Σ m_i v_i) / M_total. The total momentum of a system equals the total mass times the velocity of the center of mass: P_total = M_total v_cm.

=== CHUNK 4: Rotational Motion ===

Angular Quantities
Rotational motion is described by angular position θ (in radians), angular velocity ω = dθ/dt, and angular acceleration α = dω/dt. For constant angular acceleration: ω = ω₀ + αt, θ = θ₀ + ω₀t + (1/2)αt², ω² = ω₀² + 2α(θ − θ₀).

The relationship between linear and angular quantities for a point at distance r from the axis: s = rθ, v_t = rω, a_t = rα. The centripetal acceleration is a_c = v²/r = ω²r and points toward the center of rotation.

Torque and Rotational Dynamics
Torque is the rotational analog of force: τ = r × F. The magnitude is τ = rF sin θ. The net torque on an object equals its moment of inertia times its angular acceleration: τ_net = Iα.

The moment of inertia I depends on the mass distribution relative to the axis of rotation. For a point mass: I = mr². For a solid cylinder: I = (1/2)MR². For a solid sphere: I = (2/5)MR².

Rotational kinetic energy is K_rot = (1/2)Iω². The total kinetic energy of a rolling object is the sum of translational and rotational kinetic energy: K_total = (1/2)mv_cm² + (1/2)I_cm ω².

Angular Momentum
Angular momentum L = r × p = Iω. For a system with no net external torque, angular momentum is conserved: L_i = L_f. This explains why an ice skater spins faster when pulling arms in — moment of inertia decreases, so angular velocity must increase to conserve angular momentum.

=== CHUNK 5: Gravitation ===

Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation
Every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them: F = Gm₁m₂/r², where G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg² is the universal gravitational constant.

The gravitational potential energy of two masses separated by distance r is U = −Gm₁m₂/r. The negative sign indicates that gravity is an attractive force — work must be done to separate the masses.

Kepler's Laws
Kepler's First Law: Planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
Kepler's Second Law: A line from the Sun to a planet sweeps out equal areas in equal times (consequence of angular momentum conservation).
Kepler's Third Law: The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis: T² ∝ a³. For circular orbits: T² = (4π²/GM)a³.

The escape speed from a body of mass M and radius R is v_esc = √(2GM/R). For Earth, v_esc ≈ 11.2 km/s.
