=== CHUNK 12: Geometric Optics ===

Reflection and Refraction
Light travels in straight lines in a uniform medium. When light strikes a surface, it can be reflected or refracted. The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection: θ_i = θ_r, measured from the normal.

Snell's Law of refraction: n₁ sin θ₁ = n₂ sin θ₂, where n is the index of refraction of the medium. The index of refraction is n = c/v, where v is the speed of light in the medium. For vacuum, n = 1; for water, n = 1.33; for glass, n ≈ 1.5.

Total internal reflection occurs when light travels from a higher-index medium to a lower-index medium at an angle greater than the critical angle: θ_c = sin⁻¹(n₂/n₁). This principle is used in fiber optics.

Mirrors and Lenses
For a spherical mirror of focal length f = R/2 (where R is radius of curvature):
  1/d₀ + 1/d_i = 1/f
  Magnification: m = −d_i/d₀ = h_i/h₀

For a thin lens, the same equation holds with f positive for converging lenses and negative for diverging lenses. The lens maker's equation: 1/f = (n−1)(1/R₁ − 1/R₂).

=== CHUNK 13: Wave Optics ===

Interference
When two or more waves overlap, they interfere. Constructive interference occurs when waves are in phase (path difference = mλ). Destructive interference occurs when waves are out of phase (path difference = (m+1/2)λ).

Young's Double-Slit Experiment: Bright fringes occur at d sin θ = mλ, dark fringes at d sin θ = (m+1/2)λ, where d is slit separation. The fringe spacing on a screen at distance L is Δy = λL/d.

Thin-film interference produces the colors seen in soap bubbles and oil slicks. For a film of thickness t and index n in air, constructive interference (reflection) occurs when 2nt = (m+1/2)λ, destructive when 2nt = mλ (accounting for phase changes at boundaries).

Diffraction
Diffraction is the bending of waves around obstacles. For a single slit of width a, minima occur at a sin θ = mλ (m = ±1, ±2, ...). The central maximum has width 2λL/a.

A diffraction grating with N slits produces sharp maxima at d sin θ = mλ. The resolving power is R = λ/Δλ = mN. Diffraction gratings are used in spectroscopy to analyze light spectra.

=== CHUNK 14: Special Relativity ===

Postulates of Special Relativity
1. The laws of physics are the same in all inertial reference frames.
2. The speed of light in vacuum is the same for all observers, regardless of the motion of the source or observer.

Time Dilation and Length Contraction
A clock moving at speed v relative to an observer runs slower by a factor γ = 1/√(1−v²/c²): Δt = γΔt₀, where Δt₀ is the proper time (time in the clock's rest frame).

Length contraction: An object moving at speed v has its length contracted in the direction of motion: L = L₀/γ, where L₀ is the proper length (length in the object's rest frame).

Relativistic Momentum and Energy
Relativistic momentum is p = γmv. Relativistic kinetic energy is K = (γ−1)mc². The total energy is E = γmc². Einstein's famous mass-energy equivalence: E₀ = mc² (rest energy).

The relationship between energy and momentum: E² = (pc)² + (mc²)². For massless particles (photons), E = pc, and p = E/c.

The Doppler effect for light: f_obs = f_src √((1±β)/(1∓β)), where β = v/c. For v << c: Δf/f ≈ v/c.

=== CHUNK 15: Quantum Physics ===

Photons and the Photoelectric Effect
Light consists of quantized packets of energy called photons. The energy of a photon is E = hf, where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s is Planck's constant and f is the frequency. The momentum of a photon is p = h/λ.

The photoelectric effect: electrons are emitted from a metal surface when light of sufficiently high frequency strikes it. The maximum kinetic energy of emitted electrons is K_max = hf − φ, where φ is the work function of the metal. This demonstrated that light is quantized — wave theory could not explain the threshold frequency behavior.

Matter Waves and Wave-Particle Duality
De Broglie proposed that all matter has wave properties. The wavelength of a particle is λ = h/p, where p is momentum. Electrons can exhibit diffraction and interference, confirming their wave nature.

Light exhibits both wave properties (interference, diffraction) and particle properties (photoelectric effect, Compton scattering). This wave-particle duality is fundamental to quantum mechanics.

The Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
It is impossible to simultaneously know both the exact position and exact momentum of a particle: ΔxΔp ≥ ℏ/2, where ℏ = h/(2π). Similarly, ΔEΔt ≥ ℏ/2 for energy and time uncertainty.

=== CHUNK 16: Atomic and Nuclear Physics ===

The Bohr Model
Electrons orbit the nucleus in discrete energy levels. Transitions between levels emit or absorb photons with energy equal to the difference: ΔE = hf. For hydrogen: E_n = −13.6 eV / n², where n is the principal quantum number.

The Schrödinger Equation
In quantum mechanics, particles are described by a wave function ψ(x,t). The probability of finding a particle in a region is |ψ|²dx. The time-independent Schrödinger equation: −(ℏ²/2m)d²ψ/dx² + V(x)ψ = Eψ.

Quantum numbers describe atomic states: n (principal), l (orbital angular momentum), m_l (magnetic), m_s (spin). The Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of quantum numbers.

Nuclear Physics
The nucleus consists of protons and neutrons (nucleons). The atomic number Z is the number of protons. The mass number A is the total number of nucleons. Isotopes have the same Z but different A.

The nuclear binding energy is the energy required to separate a nucleus into its constituent nucleons. The mass defect Δm = Zm_p + Nm_n − M_nucleus is converted to binding energy via E = mc².

Radioactive decay follows N(t) = N₀e^(−λt), where λ is the decay constant and half-life T_1/2 = ln(2)/λ. Types: alpha decay (⁴He nucleus), beta decay (electron/positron emission), gamma decay (photon emission).
